Interpretation

Once your data has been collected, it's time to begin the important and often harrowing task of interpretation.

Predictions

It is always a good practice before interpreting a pattern of data to clearly articulate your predictions for each dependent measure and their interactions. These should already have been established long before data was even collected, but it is important to revisit them before thinking about whether your data can be argued to support them.

Exclusions

Another critical step prior to evaluating data is determining whether any of it needs to be excluded from interpretation. Measurements of human behaviors are often noisy, and language processing is no exception to this. A number of externalities can occur that make data difficult or impossible to interpret. Participants can be distracted by the environment, their cell phone, or their own thoughts, potentially leading them to abandon the task in the middle of a trial. It makes sense to exclude such instances, since they could bias results in a way that doesn't actually reflect the processing measure of interest. In doing this, it is VERY important to set explicit criteria for the exclusion of data BEFORE doing any interpretation. Otherwise, you risk falling into a very dangerous pattern of sculpting your data to fit your desired outcome.

Typically, any trials with reading times that exceed a certain threshold (typically RTS above 2.5 standard deviations) can be excluded from the final analysis, since they are likely not reflective of langugae processing. These are usually referred to as outliers.

Comprehension Questions

Each trial in your study should also have been paired with a comprehension question. These are an important tool for assessing whether the participant really processed the content of the trial as well as a built-in check on the quality of your materials. For example, it is typical to exclude from analysis items whose comprehension questions were answered incorrectly. Although it is unlikely if your items were well designed, you may also consider the cross-subject accuracy and exclude items for which the comprehension questions were often incorrect. This could indicate that a particular item was exceptionally confusing or difficult to interpret. In some cases, the participant's accuracy can also be a dependent measure of interest in its own right. This will depend on your particular hypotheses.

Under some circumstances, it might make sense to enclude an entire participant, if for example, they answered fewer than 80% of the comprehension questions correctly or a large proportion of their trials had to be excluded due to outlier RTs. It is again a good practice to establish exclusion criteria based on established practices in the literature before evaluating at the data.

Reading Time

Our primary measure of interest is reading time (RT). It can become somewhat counterintutive when we start linking reading times to processing speed, so it's important to remember that higher reading times map onto interference, or slower processing. Lower reading times mean faster processing.

Returning to our ongoing example experiment, let's first consider what our predictions are. Our 2x2 design means that we need to consider the main effect of each of our two independent variables, as well as their interaction.

Recall that in the restrictive condition, there is a smaller hypothesis space for upcoming words, leading to stronger predictions, which we assume facilitates processing. So, we expect that our reading times should be lower in the restrictive condition than in the non-restrictive.

We also predicted that reading times for high frequency items will be lower than for low frequency items.

What about the interaction between these conditions?

We predicted that the effect of frequency should be weaker in the restrictive condition. One explanation for this effect would be that the smaller space of hypothetical upcoming words in the restrictive condition . Predictions are much stronger, and the effects of lexical frequency is diminished.
