Self Paced Reading: How to Make Good Critical Items

Avoiding confounds

Recall how we said that we should try to balance all differences between (the critical items in the different) conditions, except for the differences that are intended by our manipulation?

This is worth stressing again, because it is very important. If our manipulations (i.e., our conditions) are confounded with other differences that plausibly could affect our outcome variable (reading times), then we won't be able to draw reliable conclusions from our experiment.

As already discussed, this means that we need to either hold constant (within any critical item) or at least balance (across all critical items) any differences across conditions. Recall the 2x2 example design we described earlier:


The example item above holds many properties that we are not interested in constant between conditions. For example, in order to manipulate contextual predictability, the item is manipulating the verb ("ate" vs. "saw"). Can you think of a reason why we didn't use "devoured" instead of "ate"? "Devoured" is much longer than "saw", whereas both "ate" and "saw" are mono-syllabic. Both verbs also have exactly three letters. Word length is a strong predictor of reading times, with longer words taking longer to read. Another reason why we don't want to use "devoured" is that "devoured" is much less frequent than "saw". Like word length, word frequency might affect reading times--as a matter of fact that's one of the hypotheses we are testing! But we are testing this hypothesis for the words "apple" vs. "kiwi", so we don't want another region in the sentence that might create an additional frequency effect. Failing to do so would mean that the effects blend together (processing difficulty with a word often spills over), which might make them harder to tease apart.

Note that similar, though slightly different, considerations apply to "apple" and "kiwi". For example, both words are bi-syllabic. They do differ in frequency, but that's ok: That's one of our intended manipulations.

The example item also holds constant between conditions many other variables known to affect reading times. For example, the target word is always equally far away from the sentence start and sentence end. Reading times are known to speed up throughout sentences, but then slow down at the boundary of clauses or sentences, as well as at line breaks, paragraph breaks, etc. The example item also holds constant the syntactic structure (and with it syntactic complexity, locality, etc.) and the position of the target word relative to that structure. All of these properties make this example item a good item.

There are many advantages to creating items that hold many properties constant. Any property that is held constant within all item is also automatically balanced across all items!

Still there are a few properties that are not held constant. For example, "saw" and "ate" might differ slighlty in frequency (feel free to look up their frequency). This is the type of difference that we might want to try to balance across items.

There are many many other differences between the conditions of the example critical item, but we only care about those that are plausible confounds. For example, "apple" and "kiwi" differ in more subtle aspects of their phonological structure. Whereas "apple" consists of a VCVC (vowel-consonant-vowel-consontant) sequence, "kiwi" consists of a CVCV sequence. It is theoretically possible that this affects reading times. But we won't worry about that because we have no reason to believe that this difference will have a particular large effect, if it has any effect at all.

Beyond confounds

In designing SPR experiments, researchers often go one step further than to just avoid confounds. Recall that it is ok, if properties vary between items, as long as it balanced with regard to the conditions. In fact a little bit of variability across items is good in that it makes the sentences less predictable and less monotonous.

On the other hand, we also don't want the different critical items to be completely different. The idea behind this is that one doesn't want to swamp the effect of interest in other differences between items that might cause much larger differences in readings. For this reason, experimenters often make critical items similar to each other. For example, we might vary the length the subject in our sentences from 1 to, say, 3 words. But we might also want to keep all verbs in all critical items transitive. Additionally, it is often a good idea to not have critical items of widely varying syntactic complexity or length.

A few final considerations for critical items suited for self-paced reading

There are a few final consideration worth mentioning that are very particular to SPR experiments:

  • Line breaks: If at all possible, we should avoid line breaks in critical items. If necessary, it is best if the sentence region of interest in the critical item is at least three words away from the beginning or end of a line.
  • End of sentence: If at all possible, the sentence region of interest should also be at least three words before the end of the sentence. The end of the sentence is often associated with large increases in reading times (presumably because of processes associated with understanding the sentence).
  • Sentence length: Unless your experiment is targeted at the consequences of complex and long sentences or for some other reason requires such sentences, it is generally a good idea to keep critical items simple (while still fulfilling the other constraints mentioned above).
